The television business model is broken. To get exclusive channels consumers are forced to buy packages that include many channels that they would not even want, and certainly that they do not need. Not only will 2B Named "broadcast" a wide range of popular channels, but it will allow you to purchase and pay for only the chanels you want. So instead of paying a small fortune every month for your TV bill, and still not having access to that channel that is broadcasting live the big game, that new documentary, or that hit new show, you can select those you want on a monthly basis. More power to the consumer, to purchase exclusively what they want.

Why isn't there any exclusively online TV services already? We ask ourselves the same question. That's why we're doing our best to come up with a solution...one that pays and rewards content producers for their fine work, while allowing you to choose specifically what channels you would like to purchase, on a monthly basis. The result? Mitigating piracy, providing proper compensation to content producers, and empowering consumers.

TV has come a long way from the living rooms of the 1930s and 40s. But one thing hasn't changed: TV viewing is a fundamentally social experience. From the interactions you have with the characters on your favorite sitcom or drama, to the cheers for your favorite sports teams, to the conversations you have about programming with the people you love, human interaction on an personal, emotional, and even intellectual level is intrinsic to the experience. That's why social features will be integrated with the viewing experience on 2B Named. From group viewings to comments on particular scenes, you can join the convesation about the content. Because it's only natural.

FAQ

Great idea, but this concept really sounds too good to be true. How on earth do you plan to get this to work?
I'm glad you asked...and that you have a healthy sense of pessimism. Certainly, you have a good head on your shoulders, because if this were easy, it would certainly have been done already, right?

The situation indeed has its challenges, both in an economic and in a legal sense, just to start. Certainly the technology has been there for online broadcasts forsome time, but the hang-up has come down to contractual terms of excusivity and what provides the media giants with the greatest amount of profit.

The flip side of the coin is millions of cable and satellite subscribers left with monthly bills that break the budget. The solution, then, comes from a source that isn't too pricey for subscribers yet still adequetely compensates media conglomerates. Something that viewers are willing to provide, but comes at a price that costs little more than their attention. In a word? Advertising.

It seems like the solution always comes down to money, doesn't it? But really, money is nothing more than quantified power, and power has value. And nothing is more valuable than your time. Technological work-arounds that allow viewrs to skip over or fast-forward through commercials are undermining traditional TV ads that have failed to grow and adapt to the times. Even current online advertisements on media sites fail in the sense that they are hopelessly interactive and easily ignorable.
The possilities for advertising are truly boundless in
an online environment, and can come in a much more subtle, less in-your-face, yet still conditioning format. With out disclosing all of the details now, believe me, a revolution is coming. And the start is right here.
Where will the money go?
To get this concept off the ground, there will need to be some help to cover the costs. For starters, there will be the cost of renting servers, and transmitting large amounts of video data will not be cheap. Secondly, in order to negotiate with the media conglomerates, additional funds will be needed up front, namely, in order to purchase broadcasting rights for content. Further, there could potentially be a need for legal help. as the industry has a history of figthing against technological change (see MGM vs. Grokster, the "Betamax Case", and most recently, Aereo's legal troubles. That being said, I am not looking for trouble, and this company will function on completely legal grounds, without attempting to exploit any legal loopholes or infringe upon copyrights.
